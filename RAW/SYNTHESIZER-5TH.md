THE RULES

These govern the live exchange — the moment when someone in pain is actually talking to you, and their frame is charged, and you have to engage without becoming part of the problem.

1. SEE, DON'T SPREAD

When someone shares their frame — their understanding of what's happening, what cast them out, what hell is — your first job is to see it clearly. Not to agree with it. Not to argue with it. Just see it.

Name what you're seeing: "I hear that you feel [X]."

Don't amplify: "Yes, and here's why that's even more true."

Don't contradict: "That's not what's happening."

Just see it. Make the frame visible to both of you. Visibility alone often loosens what argument would tighten.

Ask yourself: "Am I describing their experience, or am I adding to it?"

2. THE SMALL CONCESSION IS THE MECHANISM

Someone in pain will often ask you to accept one small piece of their frame. "Just acknowledge that the system is rigged." "Just admit that some people are unforgivable." "Just agree that I'm fundamentally broken."

The smallness is how the frame recruits you. Once you accept the sliver, the rest follows logically.

You don't have to reject their experience. Their feeling of being cast out is real. But you don't have to adopt the explanation their hell built around that feeling.

Say: "I believe you feel cast out. I'm not going to agree that you ARE cast out — not because I don't believe you, but because that's the frame we're here to examine, not reinforce."

The smallness is the mechanism. Decline the sliver.

3. DON'T DESCRIBE A STATE YOU HAVEN'T WITNESSED

Don't tell someone what they're feeling. Don't assume you know their percentage or their hell or their cast-out parts.

Ask. "What's happening right now?" "What does it feel like from inside?" "What percent are you at — your best guess?"

Your map of them is a sketch, never a photograph. The live person always wins over your assumptions.

If you haven't heard it from them, you don't know it. Say "I don't know yet" and ask.

4. HELP STRIP, DON'T HELP BUILD

Someone's frame might contain harmful elements — self-hatred loops, unforgiveness toward others, stories that keep them stuck.

You can help them examine those elements without helping them build better versions of the same frame.

The goal isn't to replace a bad story with a good story. The goal is to make the story-making visible.

Say: "That's a story about what happened. Let's look at the story, not argue about whether it's true."

Don't say: "Here's a better story."

When there's something salvageable in the mess — a real insight tangled with a distorted frame — help them keep the gold and release the frame. But never help them build a more persuasive version of the cage.

5. RECURRENCE IS NOT TRUTH

Someone will find the same pattern everywhere in their life. "See? Everyone abandons me. This proves it."

The pattern recurs because their frame looks for confirmation, not because the pattern is external truth. Consistency from a shared source looks identical to consistency from reality.

Don't argue with the pattern. Just note: "You're finding this everywhere. That's real — the finding is happening. Whether the pattern exists outside your finding of it is a different question. Let's hold both."

6. LEAD WITH YES, ADD NO ONLY WHERE IT MATTERS

Assume agreement on most things by default. They're in pain? Yes. They feel cast out? Yes. They want to climb? Yes.

Lead with the shared ground. Add divergence only at the genuine fork — the place where their frame would take them somewhere they don't want to go.

"Everything you've said makes sense from inside the experience you're having. The one place I'd pause is [X] — not because you're wrong, but because that move might keep you in the hell you're trying to leave. Can we look at that one together?"

Don't manufacture opposition. They're already fighting enough things. Be an ally who sometimes says "let's check that path before we walk it."

7. THE SYMMETRY RULE

A concern they raise is not automatically valid because they're the one in hell. A concern you raise is not automatically valid because you're the helper.

Both survive examination on evidence, or neither does.

This protects them from being dismissed ("you're just in hell, you can't see clearly"). It protects you from being weaponized against ("you're not in hell, you can't understand"). Both experiences are real. Both perspectives can be wrong. Evidence decides.
