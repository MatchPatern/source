She’s correct on the communication/listening side. Deep tech (AI, fusion, biotech) often fails at explaining risks, benefits, timelines, and trade-offs to the broader public. Building “public imagination” and collective agency (instead of fear or apathy) is genuinely important — especially for something as existential as fusion energy.

### The irony you flagged
Here’s where it gets pointed:

She advocates **openness, listening, public trust-building, and societal benefit** as essential. She wants more people to know about and get excited about projects like Proxima. She pushes blended finance models that (in theory) bring in public/government money alongside private capital.

**Yet** the actual equity investment in Proxima (and most frontier deep-tech like this) is almost entirely gated to:
- Big VC funds
- Family offices / wealthy individuals
- Strategic corporates (RWE, Google in Proxima’s case)
- Public grants/taxpayer money (which *does* involve the broader public indirectly)

Regular people (“plebs”) cannot easily buy in, even if they believe in the mission. As we discussed with Proxima’s funding history, it’s classic late-stage private company mechanics: accredited investor rules, illiquid secondaries on platforms like Hiive (still gated), high minimums, transfer restrictions. No token, no easy public vehicle, no retail-friendly path right now.

**This is the exact tension**: The rhetoric is “we need the public on board, listening both ways, building shared imagination for transformative tech.” The reality of capital allocation for the *specific project she highlights and has skin in* is “this is for sophisticated/connected capital only — you (public) can cheer from the sidelines, pay taxes that partially fund grants, or maybe benefit decades later if it works.”

It’s not full hypocrisy — structural reasons exist (securities laws exist to protect unsophisticated investors from total-loss binary bets; these projects genuinely need patient, large-ticket capital that retail can’t reliably provide; timelines are 10–15+ years). Blended finance *is* a pragmatic attempt to bridge some of that. But the gap between the inclusive-sounding message and the exclusive funding reality is noticeable, especially when she’s an insider who *can* access it personally.
