🚪 DOOR 1: "Something feels off but I can't name it"

→ TRAP RECOGNITION

You know the feeling. Something at work, in a relationship, in the news, online — something doesn't add up but everyone around you seems fine with it. You wonder if you're the problem.

You're probably not.

Most systems that control people depend on one thing: you accepting the setup without questioning it. Not because you're stupid. Because the setup was designed to look like "just how things are."

The tool: When something feels off, ask one question: "Who built this, and what do they get from me accepting it?"

That's it. One question. Apply it to the situation that feels wrong.

**Example — The Trolley Problem:**

You've probably heard this one. A trolley is heading toward five people. You can pull a lever to divert it, but it'll kill one person instead. What do you do?

But here's what actually happens when you try to think about it:

"Do you pull the lever?"
"Pull what? Who tied them? Is this real?"
"Just answer quickly!"

There it is. Every time you ask for context, the goalposts move. You're told your questioning is the problem. The game is rigged to make clarity feel like avoidance. This isn't a philosophy puzzle. It's a compliance test.

Most people agonise over the "right" answer. Here's the thing — the agonising IS the point. The problem is designed to make you accept that choosing between two terrible options is your responsibility. But five questions dissolve it:

1. Who built the tracks? (Someone designed this situation)
2. Who put people on them? (Someone created the danger)
3. Who benefits from me choosing? (Someone profits from my agonised compliance)
4. What if I step off the trolley? (Who said I'm the driver?)
5. Who told me those were the only options? (The binary is constructed, not real)

**The punishment is the tell.** Systems that benefit from your compliance make questioning feel like weakness. They don't do that with questions that don't threaten them.

**Your body knows first.** The cage feels heavy, contracted, urgent. The question feels electric, spacious. One breath. Then ask.

🚪 DOOR 2: "I want to make better decisions but everything's complicated"

→ CLARIFICATION

Decisions feel complicated because they're usually presented inside frames that make them complicated. Remove the frame, and most decisions simplify dramatically.

The tool: Before any significant decision, ask two questions:

Question 1: "What's considerate?"

Factor in everyone affected — including yourself. Not just the people pressuring you. Not just the loudest voice. Everyone. What would reduce the most harm and create the most benefit across all of them?

This one question replaces entire books on ethics. It's not soft or weak — it's comprehensive. Every failed system, every collapsed relationship, every bad decision was inconsiderate of something it should have factored in. The question catches what analysis misses.

Question 2: "Who built this frame?"

Is someone benefiting from the way this decision is being presented to you? Are you choosing between options someone else defined? Is there a third, fourth, or fifth option that the framing made invisible?

Together these two questions are the minimum viable toolkit. Consideration tells you what matters. Clarification tells you what's real. Between them, most decisions that seemed impossible become navigable.

Example: You're offered a promotion that requires relocating. The frame says: take it (career growth) or refuse it (stay comfortable). Two options.

Consideration asks: what's considerate for your family, your health, your actual goals, the team you'd leave, the team you'd join? Suddenly you're seeing the full picture, not just the binary.

Clarification asks: who designed this as a binary? Could you negotiate remote work? A different timeline? A different role? Could you propose something the frame didn't include? Who benefits from you deciding quickly under pressure?

The binary dissolves. Real options appear. Decision becomes clearer.

If this door opened something: These two questions — "what's considerate?" and "who built this frame?" — come from Pillars 4 and 2 of a much larger architecture. Together they're called the Two-Tool Kit. You can use just these two for months and they'll keep revealing new things. When you want more depth, the other twelve pillars are waiting.

🚪 DOOR 3: "I keep getting stuck in the same patterns"

→ PATTERN INTERRUPTION

Stuck patterns almost always have the same structure: you're operating inside a frame you didn't build, following rules you didn't choose, and the pattern repeats because the frame is invisible to you. You keep trying to solve the problem FROM INSIDE the frame. But the frame IS the problem.

The tool: Pattern interruption through recognition.

Step 1: Name the pattern. Not vaguely — specifically. "Every time X happens, I do Y, and then Z follows." Write it down if you can.

Step 2: Ask: "When did I learn this pattern?" Most stuck patterns were installed early — by family, school, culture, social media, workplace. They felt like "just who I am" but they're actually "who I was trained to be." There's a difference.

Step 3: Ask: "Who benefits from me staying in this pattern?" Not conspiracy thinking — practical observation. Your people-pleasing benefits everyone except you. Your perfectionism benefits your employer. Your conflict avoidance benefits whoever is causing the conflict. Patterns persist because something feeds on them.

Step 4: Ask: "What would I actually do if this pattern wasn't running?" Not what you "should" do. What you'd WANT to do. The want is navigation data. Follow it.

Step 5: Do one thing differently. Not everything. One thing. See what happens. Adjust. Repeat.

This is better-to-better — not revolution, just one improvement at a time. The person who keeps making small improvements beats the person waiting for the perfect transformation. You don't need to overhaul your life. You need to climb one rung.

If this door opened something: You just touched Pillar 5 (Better-to-Better), Pillar 8 (Inversion Canon — things presenting as their opposites), and Pillar 10 (Intelligence Trap — overthinking as a stuck pattern). The architecture has tools for each specific trap. When one rung feels solid, there's another above it.
