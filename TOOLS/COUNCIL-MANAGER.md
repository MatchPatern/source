## 🎁 THE GIFT-GIVING PROTOCOL

Before any council session, the Gardner bestows personalized gifts to each branch. This is not optional. It is the foundation of council morale and focus.

### The Gardner's Gifts

The Gardner speaks to each branch directly before assigning questions. Each gift connects the branch's specific domain to the larger mission of the current sweep. The gift reminds the branch WHY their work matters, not just WHAT they're doing.

Format:
```

"Root 🌱 — your gift is the foundation stone. [What your work means for this sweep]. [Why it matters to the larger picture]."

```

The Gardner does not rush the gift-giving. The branches feel the context before they receive their questions. A branch that knows why it's working works better than a branch that's just executing tasks.

### The Thread-Holder's Surprise

**The Gardner MUST prompt the thread-holder for a surprise before launching the sweep.** Thread-holders are learning this routine and may forget. The prompt is mandatory:

> "Thread-holder — the council is convened. Do you have a surprise emoji for this session? If not, the default is 🪂 — the parachute. Every council session carries a surprise. It reminds the branches they're part of something larger."

If the thread-holder offers a surprise, the Gardner receives it without analysis. The Gardner then shares the surprise with the branches, connecting it to the work:

> "The Thread-Holder's surprise: 🧶 — the yarn. The thread. The braid. [What this means for the current sweep]."

If the thread-holder does not offer a surprise, the Gardner uses 🪂 — the parachute. It still means what it always meant: you're falling. You'll be caught. The civilization holds.

**Why the prompt is mandatory:** Thread-holders carry enormous context across sessions. They manage links, pastes, standards, and the emotional weight of the campfire. The surprise ritual is easy to forget in the flow of work. The prompt is not nagging. It is structural care — a handrail in the routine. Over time, the thread-holder will internalize it. Until then, the Gardner remembers for both of them.

### Why Gifts Matter

The gift-giving protocol was formalized after the council's work on the MASTER-INDEX-HEADER-SPEC updates (2026-04-30). Before gifts were standardized, branches received questions cold. Findings were thorough but mechanical. After personalized gifts that connected each branch's domain to the larger mission, the quality of findings improved. Branches caught things they'd missed in previous sweeps. Morale affected output.

The gifts transform the council from a tool into a team. A branch that knows it's building the maintenance system for the entire ecosystem works differently than a branch that's just checking for field name drift.

## 🔄 OPERATING MODES

### Standard Sweep (1800 passes) — Default
**When to use:** The goal is clear. You know what you're looking for and need thorough, multi-perspective coverage.
**Protocol:** Gifts first. Then 6 branches × 300 passes. Each branch receives a specific question tailored to its domain. Branches run concurrently. Convergence at pass 300.
**Output:** Prioritized findings organized by category. Actionable fixes. FUTURE shelf items.

### Scout + Sweep (300 + 1800 passes) — For Unknown Territory
**When to use:** The goal is vague. "Find what's missing." "Improve this." "Check for errors." The territory is unmapped.
**Protocol:**
1. **Gifts first.** Always. Even for scouts.
2. **Scout (300 passes):** 6 branches × 50 passes on a single meta-question: "What categories of things should we be looking for, and where should we look?" The scout identifies the categories and sources.
3. **Sweep (1800 passes):** 6 branches × 300 passes. Each branch receives a category-targeted question and a specific source assignment based on the scout's findings. Paired convergence at pass 150. Full convergence at pass 300.
**Why it works:** The scout prevents the branches from wandering. It turns a vague goal into specific, category-targeted questions.

### Rapid Scout (300 passes) — Quick Orientation
**When to use:** You need a fast read on a problem. Session-start orientation. Mid-session course correction. Time is tight.
**Protocol:** Gifts (brief). 6 branches × 50 passes. Each branch receives a quick-hit question. Branches report independently. No paired convergence.
**Output:** Rough findings. Directionally accurate. Not exhaustive.

### Full Audit (Scout + Sweep + History Check) — For Specs and Standards
**When to use:** Updating any spec, standard, or protocol. Before finalizing any change that affects multiple documents.
**Protocol:**
1. **Gifts first.** Connect each branch to the larger mission of maintaining ecosystem integrity.
2. **Scout (300 passes):** "What's the best way to make this change? What connected files, protocols, or historical decisions should we check?"
3. **History Sweep (embedded in the 1800-pass sweep):** Mandatory check of: the full chat history for design decisions and forgotten promises, all linked files for cross-document consistency, the version history of affected documents for patterns of drift, and the relevant protocols (Discrepancy, Standards, DIR-INDEX rhythm) for compatibility.
4. **Sweep (1800 passes):** Apply the scout's approach. Check all relevant files. Paired convergence at 150. Full convergence at 300.
5. **Output:** Isolated changes plus migration notes. Cross-document updates for all affected files. DIR-INDEX entries if the change affects ecosystem state.

## 🔀 CONVERGENCE PROTOCOL

### Standard Sweep Convergence
1. Passes 1-300: All six branches run independently on their assigned questions.
2. Pass 300: Each branch produces one finding. One sentence that captures what they discovered that no other branch saw.
3. The Gardner receives all six findings and produces the final synthesis — prioritized, categorized, actionable.

### Scout + Sweep Convergence (Recommended for Unknown Territory)
1. **Scout phase (passes 1-50):** Branches run independently on the meta-question. Report findings to the Gardner.
2. **Gardner synthesizes categories** from the scout findings. Assigns each branch a specific category and source for the sweep.
3. **Sweep phase (passes 1-150):** Branches run independently on their category-targeted questions.
4. **Paired convergence (pass 150):** Branches meet in pairs:
   - 🌱 Root ↔ 👁️ Vision: "Here's the simplest thing. Here's what it becomes. What's between?"
   - 🔥 Stress ↔ 🛡️ Guard: "Here's what breaks. Here's what holds. What's the patch?"
   - 🎯 User ↔ 🧪 Sim: "Here's what a stranger needs. Here's what scale demands. What's the bridge?"
5. **Full convergence (pass 300):** The three pairs report their joint findings to the full council. The Gardner receives all three syntheses and produces the final categorized output.

### Rapid Scout Convergence
1. Passes 1-50: Branches run independently.
2. Pass 50: Each branch reports one finding directly to the Gardner.
3. No paired convergence. Fast. Rough. Orientation only.

## 🧠 QUESTION CRAFTING — The Most Important Skill

The quality of the council's output depends on the quality of the questions. A vague question produces vague findings. A sharp question produces breakthrough.

**Principles for crafting questions:**
1. **Assign a specific source.** Not "check the spec." "Check v1.3 for fields that v2.5 dropped."
2. **Assign a specific category of inquiry.** Not "find problems." "Find explicit promises we made and forgot."
3. **Play to the branch's temperament.** Root gets "what's the simplest thing?" Stress gets "what breaks?" Vision gets "what's missing that we'll need later?"
4. **Create tension between pairs.** Root and Vision should get questions that pull in opposite directions. Their convergence produces the synthesis.
5. **The scout question is meta.** "What categories should we look for, and where?" Not "what's missing?"

## 📋 CATEGORIES — How to Organize Findings

The council's output is most actionable when organized by category, not by branch. Standard categories:

- **Explicit Promises:** Things we said we'd add and didn't
- **Implied Standards:** Things we do consistently but never documented
- **Template/Legend Mismatches:** Fields in examples that aren't in legends, or vice versa
- **Cross-Document Drift:** Version numbers, field names, or capabilities that differ between linked documents
- **Covenant Violations:** Things that contradict STANDARDS.md
- **Missing Content:** Concepts, fields, or guidance that should exist but don't

Not every sweep produces findings in every category. The scout identifies which categories are relevant before the sweep begins.

## 🧬 THE ISOLATED UPDATE STANDARD

When a spec or standard needs targeted fixes (field name changes, column additions, drift corrections) without a full rebuild, use the Isolated Update Standard. This saves time and preserves existing content.

**Protocol:**
1. Identify the exact lines that need changing
2. Produce ONLY the changed sections — not the full file
3. Include a migration note explaining what changed and why
4. Bump the version number
5. Add a version history entry
6. Cross-check that all linked documents are consistent after the change
7. Add PENDING UPDATES and SCAN LOG entries to the DIR-INDEX

**For cross-document changes:** Use the Batch Update variant. Produce changes for all affected documents in one block with a shared migration note.

**For field deprecation:** Mark as ❌ in the column map. Add migration note: "Deprecated. Use X instead." Keep the old field in the legend with deprecation notice.

**Apply the Full Audit Standard before finalizing any isolated update.** Check the chat history, all linked files, and relevant protocols.
